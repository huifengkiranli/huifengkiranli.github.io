Case study - Shell Strategy Day 2021

Posted on 2021-10-06 In Strategy

Last month, after reading Shell’s strategy report and watching Shell Strategy Day 2021 presentation on YouTube. Shell as an energy company with expertise in the exploration, development, production, refining and marketing of oil and natural gas, as well as in the manufacturing and marketing of chemicals, it’s strategy delivering approach maybe helpful for other manufacturing companies. So I think it’s worth watching and writing some key points here.

Shell set out its strategy to accelerate its transformation into a provider of net-zero emissions energy products and services, powered by growth in its customer-facing businesses. Shell’s target is to be a net-zero emissions energy business by 2050, in step with society, which are clear ideas and expectations for the next decade. And delivering this strategy requires clear and deliberate capital allocation choices.

Key findings from this study:

Shell approach capital allocation at three levels: enterprise, portfolio and project:

1. At the enterprise level: increase total shareholder distributions whilst reducing net debt. Shell looks to achieve the right balance between shareholder distributions and investing for value-enhancing growth. Shell will sustain its core businesses while funding moderate growth. Having a strong balance sheet provides financial strength for resilience and allows company to pursue opportunities.

2. At the portfolio level: Shell’s strategy determines which businesses they will sustain or grow, transitioning their portfolio towards the future of energy. Through allocating capital spending between business pillars (Upstream, Transition and Growth) to achieve three key objectives: maintaining assets, sustaining cash flows from strong, existing businesses, and building new cash flows by finding and creating value in the future of energy.

Image by Shell

  • Upstream and Transition require a large proportion of capital to maintain their assets and sustain cash flows.
  • Most capital spending in the Growth pillar is aimed at increasing cash flow and value.

To accelerate Shell’s strategy to make the future of energy, the Growth pillar will have priority, attracting around half of this additional capital spending. Over time, the balance of capital spending will shift away from Upstream towards the businesses in our Growth pillar.

Customer-first strategy:

“A key principle for our strategy is our focus on the customer. This will shape the portfolios of each of our businesses, reinforcing our value over volume approach for Upstream, repurposing our refining assets for biofuels and hydrogen production, as well as developing renewable energy infrastructure and our customer portfolios.”

Image by Shell

Shell has traditionally focused on securing and developing assets, a business model that linked it to commodity prices and project-based returns. The future Shell will create value from advanced products that provide low or no-carbon energy, risk management and related services.


3. At the project level: Shell’s approach seeks to maximize value while managing risks.

Image by Shell

Shell evaluate its projects across a spectrum of criteria that align with goals: carbon, nature, people and shareholder value, in order to achieve a stable cash flow profile, while considering capital efficiency, payback periods and overall return levels.

There’s a common approach to assessing investments in projects. Nevertheless, we use different criteria to reflect the project risk and return characteristics of each business. Importantly, we assess the risk profile of each project to ensure we are achieving returns above the cost of capital, and we have an expected level of project returns for each of our businesses.

Image by Shell

  • For instance, take integrated power. Lower-risk generation assets with secure revenue streams can bear lower returns. Whereas assets with technology or market risks may need higher returns. Overall, Shell expects this integrated power business to deliver an internal rate of return above 10%.
  • Additionally, with renewable generation assets, once the asset is built, Shell’s equity investment can be reduced, while Shell still control the electrons.
  • In the Growth pillar, marketing is a lower capital intensity business where opex is also a key driver of growth. In retail, a relatively low-risk activity with fast payback, returns can reach more than 25%.
  • In the Transition pillar, returns are slightly lower and the payback periods are longer, with its Integrated Gas business providing steady cash flow streams. Importantly, these Transition businesses give Shell the infrastructure to enhance value through integration and optimisation.
  • Upstream assets are typically the most capital intensive throughout their life cycle. They also require up-front development and commodity price risk-taking. Therefore, these projects must deliver higher returns.

Ultimately, as Shell progresses towards being a net-zero energy business and its capital investment shifts towards the Growth pillar, so will its cash flows. Shell expect its cash flows will become less exposed to oil and gas prices. They will have a stronger link to broader economic growth.

Certain segments have little exposure to commodity prices and are expected to deliver growing cash flow. These include Marketing, Renewables and Energy Solutions, and Chemicals and Products. And, as one of the largest commodity traders in the world, Shell expect additional opportunities to enhance cash delivery through integration and optimisation.

I think the approach to capital allocation will define the pace and quality of strategy delivery. More importantly, Shell needs to make its choices and operational decisions in line with its strategy, pursuing shareholder returns and balance sheet strength, built upon a portfolio of high-quality, highly cash-generative assets.

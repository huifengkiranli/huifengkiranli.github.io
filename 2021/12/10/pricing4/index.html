Pricing terminology study

Posted on 2021-12-10 In Pricing

Profit drivers:

Price Sensitivity

The degree to which the price of a product influences consumers’ demand for it. Consumers are “price-sensitive” if their demand for a product changes a lot when the price changes only a little. Consumers are “price-insensitive” if their demand for the product hardly changes even if the price changes a lot.

Customer Demand

The number of units that customers would buy at a given price point.

Fixed Costs

Non-unit-based costs. A cost that does not change in direct proportion to the quantity of units produced and sold. These are often up-front costs.

For example: If you have a lemonade stand, the fixed costs include the cost to build the stand, rent the location, and advertise. However, if you were to expand into multiple locations, you would need to invest in more lemonade stands. The fixed costs would go up, but not in direct proportion to the number of cups of lemonade produced.

Variable Costs

Vary as the unit quantity a company produces and sells changes. These are the costs of each incremental unit.

For example: If you have a lemonade stand, the variable costs include everything needed to produce and sell one cup of lemonade: lemons, water, sugar, and a cup. However, these variable costs can change depending on the quantities produced. If you were to expand into multiple locations and buy lemons in larger quantities, the variable costs would go down.

Revenue

The amount of money the company takes in from selling its products. Revenue equals the price per unit paid by the customer times the total number of units sold.

Profit

The amount of money the company earns after paying all of its expenses. Profit is affected by both variables in your demand curve—the price of your product and the corresponding quantity customers will buy at that price.

Profit = Revenue - Costs (fixed costs + [variable costs x units sold])

Variable costs may change based on the quantity you produce or sell if greater quantities lead to production efficiencies that allow you to produce each unit at lower costs. Fixed costs can also change based on quantity, just not at the unit level. For example, if you increase production, you may need to add fixed costs, such as a new factory or warehousing facilities.

Value

The difference between the benefits customers receive from a product and the price they must pay to obtain it. Value is based on how well the product completes “the job” customers hire it to do. Value consists of functional benefits and symbolic benefits.

Margin

= selling price - cost to produce

Margin% = (selling price - cost) / selling price
Selling price = cost / (1-margin%)

Margin vs. Markup

when expressed in currency, they are exactly the same

  • Margin: how much money you intend to make on product
  • Markup: how much money you are making on a product
  • Markup% = (selling price - cost) / cost
  • Selling price = cost * (1+markup%)

Price discrimination

Describes the practice of selling an identical product to different buyers at different sales prices.

Price elasticity of demand

A measurement of the change in the consumption of a product in relation to a change in its price.

Peak Load Pricing

The pricing strategy wherein the high price is charged for the goods and services during times when their demand is at peak. In other words, the high price charged during the high demand period is called as the peak load pricing.

Customer value

Measures a product or service’s worth and compares it to its possible alternatives. This determines whether the customer feels like they received enough value for the price they paid for the product/service.

Next best alternative

Customers looking to buy a product usually have a specific product in mind.

Willingness to pay (WTP)

The maximum amount of money a consumer is inclined or willing to spend on a commodity. This amount can never be expressed in exact numbers as all the consumers will never have the same willingness to pay.

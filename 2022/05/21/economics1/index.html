Supply chain tracking-shipping containers availability

Posted on 2022-05-21 In Economics

Global supply chain pressures and bottlenecks becomes a huge feature in 2021, determining the pace of growth at a global level. In China, Shanghai supply chain severely strained by lockdowns in April 2022, which is showing signs of easing in early May. I want to put in some statistics behind the logistics supply chain issues by looking at the availability of containers.

So here we’re looking at the exchange container availability index, which is put together by the container exchange. This is an index where any data point above 0.5 signals more containers coming into a port than leaving a port, any data point below 0.5 is more containers leaving a port than entering a port. The link Xchange is here if you’d like to look at this yourself.

What’s interesting here, is this gray line of 2020 kind of follows the economic story rather well. In the Q3 & Q4 2020, as the US. and Europe got its footing and started to recover, and demand was starting to improve, e-commerce shot higher, demand for goods was much stronger. We saw increased container shipments out of China and there were more containers leaving China than entering China here at the port of shanghai, which makes complete sense with that economic story especially where China was the first industrial sector really get back on firm footing. By the end of the 2020, China likely worked through much of any excess inventory of containers they were holding. And so they had to wait for more containers to come back, you see this index start to tick back up towards that 0.5 level, as they have to wait for more containers to come back before they can ship out again.

If we look at the orange line for 2021 , it’s not until we get into February with the Chinese New Year, where we start to see some potential rebalancing here as more containers are coming into the Shanghai port than leaving the Shanghai port. Another way to think of this is that this could be the beginning of a rebalancing of the global supply chain, that is likely to take some time. After the seasonal impact from the Lunar New Year, we noticed that there continues to be more containers coming back to China than leaving China.

And particularly we’re focused on Shanghai here, this is likely due in part to the fact that there’s a quicker turn time for these containers in the markets of US. and the ports in Europe, as they’re unloading these containers and shipping them back empty to China to quickly be reloaded and shipped out again. And I’m sure that is a large factor in this statistic remaining above 0.5 for several months (until Q3 actually), showing shipping containers shortage in China is indeed easing.

As we move to the green line of 2022, as the severe COVID-induced lockdowns and logistic disruptions in Shanghai, more containers switch to be unloaded and reloaded in other ports in China to avoid less delivery delay. You can see the index down sharply in April, containers come to Shanghai port decrease and then up again in early May due to easing restrictions and production resumption. And I think I’m going to continue to monitor this moving forward and we’ll need to see it remain at or above that 0.5 level for some time, to really be confident to say that things are indeed easing.

But I do think that one of the stories that is happening in Q1 and will continue well into the second half of 2022, is that the reopening of western countries and then more of every dollar will be spent on services rather than goods, which could take some of the upward pressure on the demand side out of the logistics supply chain, the global supply chain pressure will dissipate over the course of 2022. So whether consumer spending is shifting back to services from goods firmly or not, it’s really something that continues and needs to be monitored closely in the near term.
